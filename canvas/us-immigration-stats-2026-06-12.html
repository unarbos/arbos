US Immigration, By the Numbers

Roughly 1 in 7 US residents was born abroad — the highest share in over a century. Immigration is now the engine of national population growth, even as the unauthorized population, the border, and the legal pipeline all sit at the center of fierce debate.

The foreign-born share peaked near 14.7% in 1890–1910, fell below 5% by 1970, and has climbed steadily ever since. Estimates vary by source and year; figures here are rounded for clarity.

Foreign-born share of US population % of total residents, 1850–2024

A classic U-shape: high during the great Ellis Island wave, bottoming out mid-century after restrictive 1920s quotas, then rising again after the 1965 Immigration and Nationality Act abolished national-origin quotas.

Mexico is still the single largest origin country (~10.6M), but its numbers have been flat-to-declining since ~2010. Asia (led by India and China) and other Latin American countries now drive most new arrivals. India and China are the top sources of recent immigrants.

The US grants ~1.1–1.2M green cards a year. The H-1B skilled-worker visa is capped at 85,000/yr and is heavily oversubscribed (selected by lottery). About 46% of immigrants are naturalized US citizens.

Encounters ≠ people: one person can be encountered multiple times, and an "encounter" includes both apprehensions and expulsions. Numbers crashed in 2020 (COVID + Title 42), spiked to record highs in FY2022–23, then fell steeply in 2024–25 after policy changes. FY2025 figures are partial/estimated.

Economic footprint

  • ~18% of the US civilian labor force is foreign-born — ~30M+ workers.
  • Immigrants are overrepresented in agriculture, construction, hospitality, health care, and STEM.
  • Foreign-born workers fill both ends: ~1/4 of US physicians and a large share of farm and home-care labor.
  • Immigrant-founded firms include a large share of Fortune 500 and major startups; CBO has credited the recent migration surge with boosting GDP and tax revenue.
  • Immigrants and their children are projected to drive nearly all future labor-force growth as native birth rates fall.

Who they are

  • ~46% of immigrants are naturalized US citizens; the rest are LPRs, temporary residents, or unauthorized.
  • ~1 in 4 US children has at least one immigrant parent.
  • Immigrants are more likely to hold a college degree than in past decades, especially Asian arrivals.
  • The unauthorized population has shifted: more visa overstays, fewer border crossers, than in the 2000s.
  • California, Texas, Florida, New York, and New Jersey host the largest immigrant populations.

The big picture

Three facts define US immigration today. First, scale: ~47.8M foreign-born residents, the most ever in absolute terms and the highest population share in over a century. Second, dependence: with fertility below replacement and deaths rising, immigration now accounts for the large majority of US population growth — without it, the country would be barely growing or shrinking. Third, contention: the legal pipeline (~1.1M green cards/yr) is dwarfed by demand, the border swung from record highs to sharp declines within two years, and an estimated 11–13M people live here without authorization — making immigration simultaneously the nation's demographic lifeline and its most polarizing policy fight.
