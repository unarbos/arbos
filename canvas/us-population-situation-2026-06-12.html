The US Population Situation

America is still growing — but only because of immigration. Births are flat, deaths are rising, the median age keeps climbing, and the country is older, slower-growing, and more dependent on migration than at any point in modern history.

The 2024 jump (+3.3M, +1.0%) reversed years of weak growth — the Census Bureau attributed nearly all of it to net international migration (~2.8M), the highest in the modern record. Natural increase (births minus deaths) was only ~0.5M.

Growth is now driven by migration, not births components of change, millions/yr

Natural increase (births − deaths) Net international migration

Natural increase has collapsed toward zero — deaths surged during 2020–2022 and births kept falling. In 2024, immigration accounted for roughly 84% of total population growth. Without it, the US would be near demographic stagnation.

An aging country share of population by broad age group

The number of Americans 65 and older has roughly doubled since 2000 and now exceeds 59 million — about 17.7% of the population. By the early 2030s, for the first time in history, older adults are projected to outnumber children under 18.

The baby boom (born 1946–1964) is fully entering retirement age, while low fertility shrinks the base of the pyramid. The result is a rising old-age dependency ratio: fewer workers supporting more retirees.

Births keep falling US births per year, millions

Annual births peaked at ~4.32M in 2007 and have declined to roughly 3.6M, even as the total population grew by tens of millions. The general fertility rate hit a record low. Each cohort entering childbearing age is having fewer children, later.

The long-running shift continues: people and political representation move toward the South and Mountain West (Texas, Florida, the Carolinas) and away from the Northeast and parts of the Midwest — though immigration helped New York and California return to growth in 2024 after pandemic-era losses.

What it all means

  • Growth is real but fragile. The 2024 rebound was driven almost entirely by immigration. Change the migration policy and the growth rate changes with it.
  • Below replacement, indefinitely. Fertility has been under the 2.1 replacement rate every year since 2007 with no sign of recovery. Long-run, this means a shrinking native-born population.
  • Aging is locked in. The 65+ share will keep rising through the 2030s regardless of policy — those people are already born. This strains Social Security, Medicare, and the labor force.
  • Regional divergence. Sun Belt states gain people, jobs, and House seats; older industrial and rural areas stagnate or shrink.
  • The open question. Whether the US continues to grow at all over the coming decades depends overwhelmingly on how much immigration it permits — the demographic equivalent of a policy dial.
